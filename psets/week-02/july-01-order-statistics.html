Core Pattern

Order-statistic problems often become easier after translating the sorted event into counts of original observations.

\[ P(X_{(k)}\le x)=P(\#\{i:X_i\le x\}\ge k). \]

For conditional laws, first describe the region. For spacings, use the fact that uniform spacings split the unit interval into random gaps.

Recognition Guide

  • For a kth value, count observations below the cutoff.
  • For two sorted values, draw \(0
  • For a conditional order law, rescale the remaining interval.
  • For exponential races, remember the minimum rate adds.
  • Uniform \(U_{(k)}\) has Beta\((k,n+1-k)\) distribution.
  • Given a maximum \(m\), the lower observations behave like uniforms on \([0,m]\).
  • Given a minimum \(a\), the upper observations behave like uniforms on \([a,1]\).
  • Joint densities usually solve range and gap questions faster than CDFs.
Problems 1-5 · Concept Builders
1. Third Order Statistic by Counting
Concept Counting CDF 7 min

Let \(X_1,\ldots,X_6\) be independent Uniform\((0,1)\). Let \(Y=X_{(3)}\). Write \(P(Y\le y)\) for \(0\le y\le1\) as a binomial tail.

Solution

The event \(Y\le y\) means at least three observations are at most \(y\). The number of observations at most \(y\) is Binomial\((6,y)\). Therefore

\[ P(Y\le y)=P(\operatorname{Binomial}(6,y)\ge3). \]

Writing the tail explicitly,

\[ P(Y\le y)=\sum_{j=3}^{6}\binom6j y^j(1-y)^{6-j}. \]
2. Beta Mean for a Quartile
Concept Beta 6 min

Let \(U_{(4)}\) be the fourth order statistic from \(9\) independent Uniform\((0,1)\) observations. Identify its beta distribution and find \(E[U_{(4)}]\).

Solution

For uniform order statistics,

\[ U_{(k)}\sim\operatorname{Beta}(k,n+1-k). \]

Here \(k=4\) and \(n=9\), so

\[ U_{(4)}\sim\operatorname{Beta}(4,6). \]

The mean of Beta\((a,b)\) is \(a/(a+b)\), hence

\[ E[U_{(4)}]=\frac{4}{10}=\frac25. \]
3. Conditional on the Maximum
Concept Conditional law 9 min

Let \(U_1,U_2,U_3\) be independent Uniform\((0,1)\), and let \(M=U_{(3)}\). Given \(M=m\), what is the conditional distribution of the smaller two sample values?

Solution

Given that the maximum equals \(m\), the other two observations must lie in \([0,m]\). By symmetry and the standard conditional order-statistic property, the smaller two sample values behave like two independent Uniform\((0,m)\) observations, sorted.

Equivalently, if the two lower values are \(V_{(1)}\le V_{(2)}\), then

\[ \left(\frac{V_{(1)}}{m},\frac{V_{(2)}}{m}\right) \]

has the same law as the order statistics of two independent Uniform\((0,1)\) observations.

4. Conditional on the Minimum
Concept Rescaling 9 min

Let \(U_1,\ldots,U_5\) be independent Uniform\((0,1)\). Given that the minimum is \(a\), find the expected maximum of the remaining four observations.

Solution

Given the minimum is \(a\), the remaining four observations behave like four independent Uniform\((a,1)\) observations. If \(Z\) is the maximum of four Uniform\((0,1)\) observations, then \(E[Z]=4/5\).

Rescale from \([0,1]\) to \([a,1]\):

\[ \text{remaining maximum}=a+(1-a)Z. \]

Therefore

\[ E[\text{remaining maximum}\mid U_{(1)}=a] =a+(1-a)\frac45 =\frac45+\frac{a}{5}. \]
5. First Exponential Arrival
Concept Exponential race 8 min

Let \(T_1,\ldots,T_n\) be independent Exponential\((\lambda)\) waiting times. Let \(T_{(1)}\) be the first arrival. Find \(P(T_{(1)}>t)\) and \(E[T_{(1)}]\).

Solution

The first arrival is after time \(t\) exactly when every waiting time is after \(t\):

\[ P(T_{(1)}>t)=P(T_1>t,\ldots,T_n>t). \]

Each term has survival probability \(e^{-\lambda t}\), so

\[ P(T_{(1)}>t)=e^{-n\lambda t}. \]

Thus \(T_{(1)}\sim\operatorname{Exponential}(n\lambda)\), and

\[ E[T_{(1)}]=\frac{1}{n\lambda}. \]

Problems 9-10 · Hidden-Pattern Problems
9. Hidden Order Statistic in a Coverage Radius
Hidden Maximum distance 22 min

A router is placed at \(0\) on a line segment \([0,1]\). Three devices are placed independently and uniformly on \([0,1]\). The router must cover the farthest device. Let \(R\) be the required coverage radius. Find \(E[R]\).

Solution

The required radius is the largest device location:

\[ R=U_{(3)}. \]

For the maximum of \(3\) independent Uniform\((0,1)\) variables,

\[ E[U_{(3)}]=\frac{3}{4}. \]

So

\[ E[R]=\frac34. \]

The hidden step is translating "cover the farthest device" into a maximum order statistic.

10. Hidden Joint Region for a Short Sample Range
Hidden ISI style 25 min

Four points are chosen independently and uniformly from \([0,1]\). Let \(R=U_{(4)}-U_{(1)}\) be the sample range. Find \(P(R\le 1/2)\).

Solution

Use the joint density of the minimum \(A=U_{(1)}\) and maximum \(B=U_{(4)}\):

\[ f_{A,B}(a,b)=4\cdot3\,(b-a)^2=12(b-a)^2, \qquad 0The event is \(b-a\le 1/2\). Let \(s=b-a\). For a fixed range \(s\), the left endpoint \(a\) can run from \(0\) to \(1-s\). Hence

\[ P(R\le 1/2)=\int_0^{1/2}\int_0^{1-s}12s^2\,da\,ds. \]

The inner integral contributes \(1-s\):

\[ P(R\le 1/2)=12\int_0^{1/2}s^2(1-s)\,ds. \]

Compute:

\[ 12\left[\frac{s^3}{3}-\frac{s^4}{4}\right]_0^{1/2} =12\left(\frac{1}{24}-\frac{1}{64}\right) =\frac{5}{16}. \]
